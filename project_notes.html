2 Lattice Networks

2.1 Theory

A symmetrical lattice is shown in figure 1 [23]. If we ignore \(Z_L\) (tend it to infinity) and \(Z_S\) (set it to zero), we can write the following transfer functions (and many more).

\[\frac {v_{out}}{v_{in}} = \frac {Z_a-Z_b}{Z_a+Z_b}\] \[Z_{in} = \frac {Z_a+Z_b}{2}\] \[Z_{out} = \frac {2Z_aZ_b}{Z_a+Z_b}\] \[Z_{trans} = \frac {v_{out}}{i_{in}} = \frac {Z_a-Z_b}{2}\]

We can derive an all-pass network using such lattices. They’re also called lattice delay networks [22].

Let us start with a case where the magnitude of the all-pass network is a constant with frequency but the phase varies in some manner that we can design. We intuit that for an all pass behaviour the transfer function must look like

\[\frac {v_{out}}{v_{in}} = \frac {1-s/a}{1+s/a} = \frac {1-Z_a/Z_b}{1+Z_a/Z_b}\]

We can implement this with an RC filter if we choose \(Z_a = R_1\) and \(Z_b = 1/sC_1\). The magnitude of the trasnfer function is 1 across all frequencies and there is a linear increase in phase shift \(\phi = -2 \arctan (\omega R_1C_1)\). This corresponds to a time delay of

\[t_d (\omega ) = \frac {-2 \arctan (\omega R_1C_1)}{\omega } \approx 2R_1 C_1 \hspace {4mm} \text {when }\omega \approx \text {low frequencies}\]

for a given frequency. A group delay can also be calculated for signals containing a spectrum

\[t_{gd} = -\frac {d\phi }{d\omega } = \frac {2R_1C_1}{1+(\omega R_1C_1)^2}\]

But the above analysis assumed that there was no source and load impedance (hence, we used the transfer functions from above). It can be shown that an RC delay lattice cannot be all-pass and have no attenuation when there is a current drawn from it. [23] shows that only an LC network can do this.

In figure 1, let’s now add back \(Z_S\) and \(Z_L\) since in actual ckts we’ll have the preceeding stage’s impedance and the load from the subsequent stage. The transfer function is now given by [23]

\[H(s) = \frac {Z_L (Z_b - Z_a)}{2Z_a Z_b + (Z_S + Z_L)(Z_a + Z_b) + 2Z_S Z_L}\]

Since we want an RC delay lattice, we can see what attenuation and \(RC\) delay we can design for given \(R_S\) and \(R_L\). Rewritng the above transfer function

\[H(s) = \frac {R_L (1 - sRC)}{(2R + R_S + R_L) + sC \big [ R(R_S + R_L) + 2R_S R_L \big ]}\]

Enforcing our all-pass condition (LHP = RHZ), we get \(R=\sqrt {R_SR_L}\), and the final transfer function is

\[H(s) = \left ( \frac {R_L}{(\sqrt {R_S} +\sqrt {R_L})^2} \right ) \frac {1 - sRC}{1 + sRC}\]

The group delay is the same as the previous analysis. We can choose \(R_L\) from the Rauch filter’s design requirements in this project. I’m not sure about \(R_S\) here.

3 Flash ADC

3.1 Theory

The sub-ADC within the front end of the pipeline is implemented with a mid-tread 9-level quantizer. The difference between a midrise vs. a midtread quantizer is shown below in figure 4 [13].

If we have 4 levels from 0V to 1V (\(V_{FS}\)), each level is 250 mV in width and the number of comparators needed is 3 since each comparator can decide whether a signal is above or below its reference level. In a single-ended case, we compare the singled ended input to each of the 3 reference values and produce a thermometer output code. Though we need 4b thermometer code to represent the output, the theoretical SQNR is that of a 2b ADC since we have only 4 levels.

What about a differential input? If the signal is at a \(V_{CM}=V_{DD}/2\) then the differential signals are \(V_{DD}/2 \pm v_{id}/2\). The differential signal (\(v{id}\)) itself can swing from \(-V_{DD}\) to \(V_{DD}\). Unlike the single ended case, we now have to accommodate \(2V_{FS}\) in the same number of levels. The quantization step increases by a factor of 2, i.e., \(v_{id}\) can only be discretized in 500 mV steps with the 4 level quantizer. The SQNR doesn’t change since both the signal and quanitization noise power have gone up by the same amount \(\times 4\).

[14] goes through how to generate the reference voltages in the differential cases. Essentially, we compare \((v_{ip}-v_{ref,p})\) vs \((v_{in}-v_{ref,n})\) using the comparators. In our case, we have 9 levels with midtread quantization. Figure 2 shows the mapping from thermometer code to binary code (two’s complement). We essentially have 3b resolution in terms of quantization steps and the MSB is used for the sign.

3.3 Comparator Design

Since we have a low supply voltage, we’d like to avoid stacking tranistors or voltage boosting techniques for the comparator design. Although the cross-coupled pair is not in saturation we care about the supply voltage for the following reason [1]. In figure 7, in the StrongArm latch, at the beginning of the decision, only the devices M3 and M4 start pulling the output nodes down. The regeneration fully kicks in when the output nodes fall below sufficiently to turn on M5 and M6. If the supply voltage is lowered, the \(V_{GS}\) on these devices is lowered and it takes longer for M5 and M6 to turn on.

The double tail comparator solves this issue by separating the sampling and regeneration stages. The output nodes can now swing lower because the source of the NMOS devices of the latching stage are at GND and not the \(V_{DS}\) of the input pair. This separation also means we can have a higher tail current in the latching stage to allow for faster settling, and a smaller current in the sampling stage for lower offset [1]. I do not understand fully why the comment about offset is true (todo).

We will implement the double tail comparator for its benefits over the StrongArm such as higher sampling rates, lower kickback noise, and being able to run at lower supply voltages without increasing delay (or compromising on sampling rate).

3.3.1 Double Tail Comparator

We follow the analysis from [1]. The operation of the comparator is as follows. During the reset phase (clk low, \(M_{tail}\), \(M_{tail2}\) are off), devices M3 and M4 precharge the nodes \(f_p\) and \(f_n\) to VDD. This in turn causes the devices MR1 and MR2 to be turned on and discharge the output nodes to GND. During the decision making phase (clk is high and \(M_{tail}\), \(M_{tail2}\) are on), M3 and M4 are off and the nodes \(f_p\) and \(f_n\) start to discharge. This discharge rate has two terms - one that is directly proportional to \(I_{tail1}/C_{fnp}\) and another proportional to the differential voltage at the input pair. The difference is therefore \[\frac {d V_{fp}}{dt} = \frac {I_{tail1}/2+g_{m12}v_{d}/2}{C_{fp}} \] \[\frac {d V_{fp}}{dt} = \frac {I_{tail1}/2-g_{m12}v_{d}/2}{C_{fp}} \] \[\frac {d \Delta V_{fpn}}{dt} = \frac {g_{m12}v_d}{C_{fp}} \]

Say the time taken for the devices M9 and M10 to turn on (i.e., for \(out_p\) and \(out_n\) to rise to \(V_{th}\)) is \(t_0\). We can approximate this as [1]

\[t_0 \approx \frac {V_{thn}C_{Lout}}{I_{tail2}/2}\]

Since we know that \(I_{tail2}\) is steered by \(g_{mR12}\Delta V_{fpn}\) to be asymmetric in either branches, we can write the difference in output voltages as

\[\Delta V_{opn} \approx 2V_{thn}\frac {g_{mR12}\Delta V_{fpn}}{I_{tail2}} = 2V_{thn}\frac {g_{mR12}\Delta V_{fpn}}{I_{tail2}} \cdot t_0 \cdot \frac {g_{m12}v_d}{C_{fp}} = \biggl ( \frac {2V_{thn}}{I_{tail2}}\biggr )^2 \cdot \frac {C_{Lout}}{C_{fnp}} \cdot g_{m12}g_{mR12} \Delta v_{in}\]

We see that we want a high \(g_{mR12}\) and a high \(\Delta V_{fpn}\) to maximize this \(\Delta V_{opn}\) just before the regeneration starts, and a high \(I_{tail2}\). We can show that the regeneration delay itself (i.e., once xcoupled devices have turned on) is given by

\[t_{latch} = \frac {C_{Lout}}{g_{meff}} \cdot \ln \bigg ( \frac {V_{os}}{\Delta V_{opn}}\bigg )\]

where \(V_{os}\) is the total output swing (which [1] assumes to be \(V_{DD}/2\)) and \(g_{meff}\) is the ‘effective’ transconductance of the xcoupled devices. I’m not too sure what this effective \(g_m\) is but my guess is that increasing \(g_m\) of the individual devices in the xcoupled invereters might improve this (todo: there is a reference in [1] that might be helpful in understanding this latch delay further).

The final delay for a swing of \(V_{DD}/2\) is given by (the swing itself doesn’t matter too much since it’s inside a logarithmic function)

\[t_{delay} = t_0 + t_{latch} = 2\frac {V_{thn}C_{Lout}}{I_{tail2}} + \frac {C_{Lout}}{g_{meff}} \cdot \ln \biggl ( \frac {V_{DD} I^2_{tail2} C_{fnp}}{kV^2_{thn}C_{Lout} g_{m12} g_{mR12} \Delta V_{in}}\biggr )\]

3.3.2 Design Specs

Let’s say we have a \(V_{DD}\) of 1 V. The smallest \(\Delta V_{in}\) we need to resolve is 125 mV since this is the LSB of the coarse ADC. Since the sampling rate is 800 MS/s and we need to make a decision within half a clk cycle, we have 625 ps for the comparator output to settle. The comparator has to drive the output of a therm2bin converter (which is ideal Verilog-A in our case). I will assume that a fat tree structure is being used to implement this encoder and then model the first logic gate with LE = 2 (reasonable) and using min. sized devices. We also need to drive the DAC switches which are modeled as 2 inverters as seen in figure 13. Therefore, the \(C_{Lout}=4C_{inv}\) where the inverter is made of min. sized devices. We can set the input common mode to be midrail at 500 mV. For ease of design we set \(g_{mR12}=g_{m12}\).

Once we get the minimum \(g_m\) and \(I_{tail2}\) for the first and second stages respectively, we can size them up so that we minimize the IRN. 3.4 Flash ADC using Double Tail Comparator

We now implement the 9-level flash ADC using the double tail comparator. We need to compare the differential input to the differential reference voltages and then generate a code. \[v_{inp} = V_{CM} + v_{id}/2\] \[v_{inn} = V_{CM} - v_{id}/2\] \[v_{refp,k} = V_{CM} + v_{ref,k}/2\] \[v_{refn,k} = V_{CM} - v_{ref,k}/2\]

\[\text {Evaluate }(v_{inp} - v_{refp,k}) - (v_{inn} - v_{refn,k} )\]

Note that a \(V_{CM}\) needs to be added in the above evaluation simply to bias the input pair. To subtract these voltages we create a switched capacitor based sampling circuit as shown in figure 2. Phase 2 corresponds to clk and phase is clkb. One drawback is that we need to keep resetting the capacitor at every cycle. Another way to evaulate the same expression is to make the exisitng double tail comparator into a differential topology. We instead evaluate \[(v_{inp} + v_{refn,k}) - (v_{inn} + v_{refp,k} )\]

The sizing of the input pair need not be changed even though each device now has \(g_m/2\), there are two of them in parallel in each branch.

This is implemented with min. sized devices except for the devices for \(I_{tail1/2}\) which are twice the size to carry the currents from two transistors in the branch without sacrificing \(g_m\). To reduce the noise injected from the transient spikes into the refernce voltages through the gates of the devices (\(C_{gs}\), \(C_{gd}\) during clk transitions), a 1 pF decap was added at each reference node in the resistor ladder tail [20]. 10 ps transient clk and aF of capacitance gave around a few \(\mu \)A of gate currents which across a few 100 k\(\Omega \) resistance gave spikes in reference voltage of hundreds of mV during the clk transitions. One issue with the decap is that the references take time to settle and the first couple of clk cycles might have to be ignored. Adding buffers can also work and I tried this with ideal VCVS, and things worked. Another note about the ladder is that since this is a mid-tread quantizer, we cannot have 9 unit resistors but instead have 7 unit resistors and \(R/2\) resistors at the very top and bottom.

4 Switched-Resistor DAC

4.1 Theory

As mentioned above, the DAC is switched half a clk cycle after the ADC to give it time to settle. The DACs in the first stage are virtual-groung-switched NRZ DACs [12]. Let’s try to understand the DAC in figure 13.

The key point here is that the DAC is connected to the virtual GND node of the filter. The output sequence can be converted to a current using a resistor as shown on the left in figure 14.

If the output of the ADC is a \(M\)-bit thermometer code, we can drive \(M\) unit elements each of resistance \(M\cdot R\) to get a full-scale current (when all \(M\) elements are on) of \(M\cdot V_{DD}/MR = V_{DD}/R\) [13]. The same idea is extended to a differential case where the resistors are switched between the two references as shown on the right in figure 14 (which is the same as figure 13 with the negative reference at GND). In the differential case, the maximum noise spectral density is given by \(4kT/R + 4kT/R\) (variances add up) and when referred to the input, we get a noise voltage PSD of \(8kTR\) [13]. It can be shown that switched-resistor DACs have the smallest noise contribution for the same full-scale current delivered. One drawback of this DAC, however, is that the resistances now load the OTA input and can cause degradation in loop-gain. This effects the linearity of the integrator in a CT-DSM and the loop filter here in the CTP.

4.2 NRZ DAC

An NRZ DAC holds the voltage corresponding to the bits that are turned on for the entire clock cycle. A RZ DAC on the other hand delivers a pulse only for half the clock cycle (with double the amplitude to keep the average energy the same/area under the curve). The NRZ DAC in our paper is clocked at the falling edge w.r.t the comparator in the flash ADC. Say the ADC is clocked at \(t=0\) and the DAC is then clocked at \(t=0.5T_{clk}\). The pulse is on till \(t=1.5T_{clk}\). We can approximate this as a delta pulse of the same magnitude at \(t=T_{clk}\). Therefore, the paper claims to have an ‘effective’ delay of \(T_{clk}\) in the flash-DAC path.

5 Biquad Filter

The paper implemented a DC gain of 4, maximally flat biquad with cut-off frequency at 110 MHz.

5.1 Theory

The biquad we’re interested in is of the form [17]

\[H(s) = \frac {K.\omega ^2_n}{s^2+\frac {\omega _n}{Q}s+\omega ^2_n}\]

where \(K\) is the pass band gain, \(\omega _n\) is the natural frequency, and \(Q\) is the quality factor. As \(Q\) tends to infinity, we get an oscillator as the poles are now at \(\pm \omega _n\). A larger \(Q\) can, however, be useful in getting a steepr drop-off in the transition region from pass band to stop band for the same order. The trade-off is, of course, stability of the filter. We get a maximally flat response (Butterworth filter) when \(Q=1/\sqrt {2}\) and the cut-off frequency matches \(\omega _n/2\pi \).

5.4 Replacing the ideal VCVS

We now have to replace the ideal VCVS with an OTA. The paper uses a 3-stage feedforward compensated OTA. The specs to meet here are 54 dB DC gain and 2 GHz unity gain frequency. There are two feedforward paths, one from the input to the output of second stage and the other that is from the input to the output. This path is capacitively coupled. The first stage also has a negative impedance to boost its DC gain. Let us first understand the topology before getting into the fine details.

5.5 Feedforward compensation

The idea of feedforward compensation is to place a LHP zero so that the root locus plot at high DC forward gain does not run away to infinity along a line parallel to the imaginary axis, but it actually turns back down and one branch ends on it while the other runs away to -ve infinity on the real axis. Some advantages of this compensation compared to Miller compensation are [7], [6]

1.
Higher unity gain frequency for the same power.
2.
More stable for unity gain feedback, and as feedback factor decreases the phase margin decreases as well.
3.
We can increase phase margin without burning more power by simply adding additional loading capacitors in the intermediate stages.

A big disadvantage, however, is that it has slow settling transients. This is because the loop gain’s poles remain at the same place unlike the splitting that happens in Miller compensation. So once the fast path from the unity gain frequency does its job, the slower two-pole response slowly reaches the final value. It is, therefore, not used a lot in opamps where settling time matters (such as discrete-time circuits). In CT ADCs, this is a good topology since we don’t care much about step response settling, the entire waveform matters [13].

9 Digital Reconstruction Filter

9.1 Theory

[2] describes the design of the Digtial Reconstruction Filter (DRF). Figure 25 shows the block diagram of a single stage CTP with the DRF. A standard quantizer has the following model

\[v[n] = y(nT_s) + q[n]\]

where \(y(nT_s)\) is the sampled input nd \(q[n]\) is the quantization noise. The DAC output for a sequence \(v[n]\) and with a transfer function \(p(t)\) is given by

\[v(t) = \sum _{n}^{} v[n] \, p(t-nT_s) = v_1(t) \star p(t)\]

where \(v_1(t)\) is the CT function of an input sequence \(v[n]\) and is given by \(v_1(t) = \sum _{n}^{} v[n] \delta (t-nT_s)\).

Say we now have an input \(u(t)\) to the CTP in figure 25. The input of the residue amplifier is simply \(u(t-t_d)\) where \(t_d\) is the delay introduced by the delay line, and the coarse approximation through the ADC\(_1\)-DAC\(_1\) path. If the output of ADC\(_1\) is \(v_1[n]\) (not the intermediate \(v_1(t)\) from above), we can write

\[v_1[n] = u(nT_s) + q_1[n]\]

Assuming an NRZ DAC (\(p(t) = 1\) for the full clk cycle), we can write the output of DAC\(_1\) as

\[v_1(t) = \sum _{n}^{} v_1[n] \, p(t-nT_s) = \sum _{n}^{} (u(nT_s) + q_1[n]) \, p(t-nT_s) = \sum _{n}^{} u(nT_s) \, p(t-nT_s) + \sum _{n}^{} q_1[n] \, p(t-nT_s)\]

\(p(t)\) can also be written as \(p(t) = \text {step}(t)-\text {step}(t-T_s)\) or \(p(t) = \text {rect} \bigg (\frac {t-\frac {T_s}{2}}{T_s}\bigg )\)

At the summation node of the residual amplifier, we have

\[r(t) = u(t-t_d) - v_1(t) = u(t-t_d) - \sum _{n}^{} u(nT_s) \, \text {rect} \bigg (\frac {t-\frac {T_s}{2}}{T_s}\bigg ) - \sum _{n}^{} q_1[n] \,\text {rect} \bigg (\frac {t-\frac {T_s}{2}}{T_s}\bigg )\]

Say the biquad has the impuls response \(h_1(t)\) and a DC gain of \(G_1\). The output of this block is now

\[y_b(t) = r(t) \star G_1\cdot h_1(t)\]

The output sequence at the backend quantizer is

\[v_b[n] = y_b(nT_s) + q_b [n] = r(t) \star G_1\cdot h_1(t) |_{t=nT_s} + q_b [n] \]

The above convolution is evaluated at \(t=nT_s\). The output sequence at the backend quantizer is

\[v_b[n] = \bigg (u(t-t_d) - \sum _{k}^{} u(kT_s) \, \text {rect} \bigg (\frac {t-\frac {T_s}{2}}{T_s}\bigg ) - \sum _{k}^{} q_1[k] \,\text {rect} \bigg (\frac {t-\frac {T_s}{2}}{T_s}\bigg )\bigg ) \star G_1\cdot h_1(t) |_{t=nT_s} + q_b [n]\]

To reconstruct the true output value, we pass the sequence \(v_1[n]\) through a discrete time version of the impulse response of the filter.

\[v_{1f} [n] = v_1[n] \star G_1 h_1[n] = (u(nT_s) + q_1[n]) \star G_1 h_1[n] \]

The final output is

\[v_o [n] = \frac {1}{G_1}(v_{1f}[n] + v_b[n]) = \frac {1}{G_1} (v_1[n] \star G_1 h_1[n] + v_b[n]) \]

We can simplify this to

\[ \begin {split} v_o [n] &= u(nT_s) \star h_1[n] - \sum _{k}^{} u(kT_s) \text {rect} \bigg (\frac {t-\frac {T_s}{2}}{T_s}\bigg ) \star h_1(t) \bigg |_{t=nT_s} + u(t-t_d) \star h_1(t) \bigg |_{t=nT_s} \\ &\quad + q_1[n] \star h_1[n] - \sum _{k}^{} q_1[k] \text {rect} \bigg (\frac {t-\frac {T_s}{2}}{T_s}\bigg ) \star h_1(t) \bigg |_{t=nT_s} \\ &\quad + \frac {1}{G_1} q_b[n] \end {split} \]

While the above expression does look complicated, we make some observations to simplify it. In the expression \(\sum _{k}^{} u(kT_s) \text {rect} \bigg (\frac {t-\frac {T_s}{2}}{T_s}\bigg ) \star h_1(t) \bigg |_{t=nT_s}\), \(u{kT_s}\) is a constant. Therefore, we can pull the \(\text {rect} \bigg (\frac {t-\frac {T_s}{2}}{T_s}\bigg )\) out and convolve with \(h_1(t)\) as shown below.

\[\sum _{k}^{} u(kT_s) \text {rect} \bigg (\frac {t-\frac {T_s}{2}}{T_s}\bigg ) \star h_1(t) \bigg |_{t=nT_s} = \sum _{k}^{} u(kT_s) \cdot (\text {rect} \bigg (\frac {t-\frac {T_s}{2}}{T_s}\bigg ) \star h_1(t) \bigg |_{t=nT_s})\]

We notice that \(\text {rect} \bigg (\frac {t-\frac {T_s}{2}}{T_s}\bigg ) \star h_1(t) \bigg |_{t=nT_s}\) is just the discrete time convolution and the whole expression can be simplified to

\[\sum _{k}^{} u(kT_s) \text {rect} \bigg (\frac {t-\frac {T_s}{2}}{T_s}\bigg ) \star h_1(t) \bigg |_{t=nT_s} = \sum _{k}^{} u[n] \cdot g[n-k]\]

where \(g[n]\) is the discrete time convolution of the DAC’s and filter’s responses \(g(t) = p(t)\star h_1 (t) |_{t=nT_s} \). But we now see that the above summation is, once again, just a discrete time convolution.

\[\sum _{k}^{} u[n] \cdot g[n-k] = u[n] \star g[n] = u(nT_s) \star g[n]\]

The final output sequence expression now simplifies to

\[v_o[n] = u(nT_s) \star (h_1[n]-g[n]) + u(t-t_d) \star h_1(t) \bigg |_{t=nT_s} + q_1[n] \star (h_1[n]-g[n]) + \frac {1}{G_1} q_b[n]\]

To obtain the result in paper [2], we need to program \(h_1[n] = g[n] = g(t) = p(t)\star h_1 (t) |_{t=nT_s}\). This is accomplished in the paper by the function c2d as follows. The c2d function in MATLAB implments a zero order hold, which essentially implements an NRZ DAC as shown in figure 26 [9] [10]. Please note the table in [10] that explains when a given method is applicable.

To get a discrete time equivalent of \(H(s)\), MATLAB implements an NRZ DAC to sample and hold and this is exactly our signal chain from the output of ADC\(_1\) to the resiude amplifier. Therefore, \(h_1[n]\) and \(g[n]\) cancel out yielding the following expression.

\[v_o[n] = u(t-t_d) \star h_1(t) \bigg |_{t=nT_s} + \frac {1}{G_1} q_b[n]\]

The wonderful result here shows that the quantization noise of ADC\(_1\) is cancelled by the digital reconstruction!

9.2 IIR Filters

We need to now implement c2d(\(G_1H_1(s)\)). We use the ZOH model since we want to include the effect of the DAC’s TF. The DAC TF is given by

\[P(s) = \frac {1-\exp (_sT_s)}{s}\]

We take the inverse Laplace transform of \(G_1H_1(s)\) with \(P(s)\) sampled at \(nT_s\) and then apply the Z-transform on this sampled sequence.

\[\tilde {H}(z) = \mathcal {Z} \biggl \{ \mathcal {L}^{-1} \biggl \{\frac {1-\exp (-sT)}{s} \, G_1H_1(s) \biggr \} \bigg |_{t=nT_s} \biggr \}\]

We can simplify this

\[\tilde {H}(z) = (1-z^{-1}) \mathcal {Z} \biggl \{ \mathcal {L}^{-1} \biggl \{ \frac {G_1H_1(s)}{s} \bigg |_{t=nT_s} \biggr \} \biggr \} \]

Running c2d_biquad.py we can find \(\tilde {H}(z)\)

\[\tilde {H}(z) = \frac {0.9758 z^{-1} + 0.6455 z^{-2}}{1 - 0.8894 z^{-1} + 0.2947 z^{-2}}\]

I implemented the above IIR filter (btw it is IIR because there are poles which are not only at zero) in Cadence and tested it on Python too. 9.3 FIR Filters

\(G_1H_1[z]\) is an IIR filter ideally but we can only implement FIR filters in HW [2]. Figure 28 shows an FIR filter in its standard form and an alternate lattice form [21]. Each coefficient \(b_n\) is finite and the filter involves no feedback (inherently stable) unlike an IIR filter.

The impulse response of a (causal) FIR filter is then

\[h[n] = \sum _{i=0}^{N} b_i \cdot \delta [n-i] \hspace {4mm} \forall \, 0 \geq n \leq N\]

Taking it’s Z-transform, we get

\[\tilde {H}(z) = \sum _{i=0}^{N} b_i \cdot z^{-i} = \frac {b_0 z^N + b_1 z^{N-1} + \cdots + b_N}{z^N}\]

We see that the transfer function has \(N\) poles at z=0 and the numerator gives \(N\) zeros. We can have complex zeros but they must come in complex conjugates if we insist all \(b_i\) be real. Note that in IQ-mixing, we can use complex coefficients too. Let’s take a simple example to derive some intuitions [5].

9.3.1 A Low-Pass Filter using FIR Filters

In the z-plane, z=1 corresponds to DC or \(f_s\) and z=-1 to \(f_s/2\). We can traverse the circle and see that \(z=\pm i\) correspond to \(f_s/4\) and \(3f_s/4\) respectively. If we want a LPF, we need to kill high frequency gain so we want a zero in the LHP (not to be confused with LHP on s-plane!). Let’s look at an example with a 2-tap FIR filter and study it’s magnitude and phase response as a function of coefficients.

\[\tilde {H_2}(z) = \frac {b_0z + b_1}{z}\]

\[|\tilde {H_2}(z)| = \sqrt {b^2_0 + b^2_1 +2b_0b_1 \cos (\omega /f_s)}\]

\[\arg \tilde {H_2}(z) = \arctan \bigg (\frac {b_1 \sin (\omega /f_s)}{b_0 + b_1 \cos (\omega /f_s)} \bigg )\]

The zero is located at \(z=-b_1/b_0\). For the magnitude of the TF to be less than 1, we want \(|b_0| + |b_1| \leq 1\) and we can extend this to N-tap filters too.

From figur 29, we see that when \(b_0\) and \(b_1\) have the same sign, we get a low-pass filter (zero is on the LHP) and for opposite signs, we get a high-pass filter (zero is on the RHP).

So far looks like the zeros of the transfer function steal the show. [5] comments on how the poles affect the magnitude.

1.
A pole on the unit circle will blow up the magnitude response at the frequency
2.
A zero on the unit circle will null the magnitude but as the zero moves away from the circle and towards the origin (as seen in the third example in figure 29), the frequency response increases (it’s not nulled anymore)
3.
Similarly, if a pole moves away from 0 to 1 the DC response increases and the response at \(f_s/2\) decreases
4.
The idea is that it’s the distance between the poles and zeros that determines the magnitude response (and which plane they sit in determines the phase response). In the 2-tap FIR filter results above, the pole was always at zero, so as the zero of the TF moved around, we got different magnitude responses

FIR filter with symmetric coefficients can give low-pass filters and are called type-1 FIR filters [3]. A couple of results are plotted in figure 30.

10.2 How to add the outputs?

Going back to the equation for the final, processed output sequence,

\[v_o[n] = u(t-t_d) \star h_1(t) \bigg |_{t=nT_s} + \frac {1}{G_1} q_b[n]\]

we see that the effective resolution depends on the backend ADC and the gain of the residue amplifier. Say the backend ADC has \(B_2\) bits. The quantization noise of the backend amplifier is bounded by \(\pm V_{FS}/2^{B_2}\). Thanks to the gain from the amplifier, the final output has a lower quantization noise and has an effective resolution of \(B_2 + \log _2(N)\). [15] goes through the derivation. The first stage’s resolution only sets the maximum gain that the residue amplifier can have (higher resolution of the sub-ADC means we can have a higher gain on the filter before it saturates the input of the backend ADC) [16]. In our case, this corresponds to an output with \(128 \times 4 = 512\) levels, or 9-bit output.

We have the following signal chain - a 9-level sub-ADC with a differential LSB of \(V_{FS}/4\), a DAC with the same LSB as the sub-ADC, a BE ADC with differential LSB \(V_{FS}/64\) width, and a biquad and digital IIR filters with a gain of 4. To add two binary sequences, we need to make sure that their LSBs physically represent the same value. The 6b output of the IIR filter still has \(V_{FS}/8\) (or \(V_{FS}/4\) differentially) the LSB. The BE ADC has an LSB of \(V_{FS}/128\). To align the outputs, we have to left shift the IIR filter output by 4 and then add.

\[G_1 \cdot v_o[n] = G_1 \cdot ( u(t-t_d) \star h_1(t) \bigg |_{t=nT_s} ) + q_b[n]\]

means that we can right shift the 6b output by 2 (since \(G_1=4\)) and then add to 7b output from the backend.

\[f \begin {array}{rcccccccccl} & \text {MSB} & & & & & & & \text {LSB} & \\ \text {Filtered FE (6-bit)} & F_5 & F_4 & F_3 & F_2 & F_1 & F_0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & (\times 2^3) \\ \text {Backend ADC (7-bit)} & + & &B_6 & B_5 & B_4 & B_3 & B_2 & B_1 & B_0 \\ \hline \text {Final Result (9-bit)} & D_8 & D_7 & D_6 & D_5 & D_4 & D_3 & D_2 & D_1 & D_0 & \end {array} \]

[15] shows that the redundancy we get actually helps correct any errors due to comparator offsets in the sub-ADC in the DT pipeline case.

10.3 Timing

Let’s start with the rising clk edge at clk cycle 0 and trace when the outputs from different blocks are valid and what are they quantizing exactly. At the posedge of clk 0, the input \(u(t)\) is quantized by the sub-ADC. The sampling phase starts at the posedge and there is a clk-to-Q delay (which is currently set to \(0.25 T_{clk}\)) after which the comparator makes the decision. The decision is immediately relayed to the DAC. The DAC turns on at clkb - need to figure out how to get it to trigger at the posedge of clkb, maybe register the sub-ADC output? The sub-ADC’s output is registered so that the quantized value is valid at posedge of clk cycle 1. The DAC is give half a clk cycle to settle. So once the sub-ADC’s output drives the DAC at clkb, the subtraction happens starting from posedge of clk cycle 1. The input that the sub-ADC quantized is thus, delayed by a clk cycle so that the resiude generated is relevant to the data at clk 0 posedge. The residue for \(u(t-t_d)\) is generated from clk 1 posedge to the end of that clk cycle. The BE ADC (or the next stage) now quantizes this value at the posedge of clk 2.
